Lesson 2c: Vertical Boundaries (Make vs. Buy Decision)

This topic addresses one of the most fundamental strategic questions a firm faces: Which activities should we perform ourselves, and which should we outsource to independent firms in the market? The answer defines the firm's vertical boundaries.

Lesson 2c: Vertical Boundaries (Make vs. Buy Decision)

This section examines a firm's decision regarding which steps in the vertical production chain to perform internally ("make") versus acquiring from the market ("buy") and the economic factors that drive this choice, particularly transactions costs and relationship-specific assets.

1. Detailed Explanation

The Vertical Chain of Production describes the entire process a product goes through, from raw materials to the final sale to the consumer. Early steps are called upstream (e.g., extracting oil), and later steps are called downstream (e.g., running a gas station).

The Make-or-Buy Decision is the choice a firm makes at each step of this chain:

  • "Make" (Vertical Integration): The firm performs the activity in-house. This means owning and controlling that part of the production process. If a firm expands its ownership to control earlier steps in the chain, it's called backward integration. If it expands to control later steps, it's called forward integration.
  • "Buy" (Using the Market/Outsourcing): The firm purchases the good or service from an independent firm.

The decision is not simply about what is cheapest on paper; it is a complex strategic choice that involves balancing costs, capabilities, and risks. The framework for this decision rests on comparing the benefits and costs of using the market versus the benefits and costs of vertical integration.

A. Reasons to "Buy" (Use the Market)

Market firms (specialized suppliers) often have significant advantages that make "buying" the better choice.

  • Exploiting Economies of Scale and Learning Curves: This is the most powerful argument for using the market. A specialized supplier, like a tire manufacturer, produces for many different car companies. By aggregating this demand, it can achieve a much larger scale of production (the Minimum Efficient Scale) and a steeper learning curve than a single car company could by making its own tires. This results in substantially lower per-unit costs.
  • Market Discipline: Competition in the open market forces suppliers to be efficient, innovative, and cost-conscious. An internal division of a large company does not face this same competitive pressure. It has a guaranteed "customer" (its parent company) and can become complacent, inefficient, and less innovative over time, a problem known as "bureaucratic slack."

B. Reasons to "Make" (Vertically Integrate)

While the market offers efficiency, it is not costless or frictionless. The costs of using the market are called Transaction Costs. When these costs are high, it becomes more attractive to "make."

  • High Transaction Costs: These are the "hidden" costs of doing business with an outside firm, including:
    • The time and expense of searching for suppliers.
    • Negotiating and writing complex contracts.
    • Monitoring the supplier's quality and performance.
    • Enforcing contracts if a dispute arises.
    If these costs become too burdensome, performing the activity in-house can be simpler and cheaper.
  • The Hold-Up Problem & Relationship-Specific Assets: This is the most critical reason for vertical integration.

    A Relationship-Specific Asset is an investment made to support a particular transaction that has little value outside of that specific relationship. For example, a supplier buys a unique mold that can only produce a custom-designed component for a single client, like Boeing. This investment is now a sunk cost tied to the Boeing relationship.

    The Hold-Up Problem arises after this investment is made. The client (Boeing) now has leverage. They can demand a lower price, knowing the supplier is "stuck" with the investment and cannot easily sell the component to anyone else. The supplier is vulnerable to being "held up."

    Consequence: Fearing this, a rational supplier might refuse to make the best-possible investment in the first place, leading to lower quality or higher costs for everyone. To avoid this entire problem and encourage the necessary specific investment, the firm may choose to make the component itself (vertically integrate).

  • Coordination and Quality Control: For complex products that require a perfect fit and precise timing between components (e.g., an aircraft engine), relying on outside suppliers can be risky. Bringing production in-house gives a firm direct control over quality, scheduling, and design, ensuring all the pieces fit together seamlessly.
  • Protecting Proprietary Information: Outsourcing a critical process might mean sharing trade secrets or proprietary technology with a supplier. This creates a risk that the supplier could leak that information to competitors or become a competitor themselves in the future.

2. Examples

Examples of the "Make" Decision (Vertical Integration):

  • Boeing: As detailed in the article "Plane Makers Want a Bigger Part of Parts," Boeing has been moving to produce more of its own components in-house. The reasons are a perfect illustration of the theory: to reduce the power of large, consolidated suppliers (avoiding the hold-up problem), to gain more control over the quality and timing of critical parts, and to capture more of the profit from the lucrative aftermarket for spare parts.
  • Tesla: Tesla is famously vertically integrated. It not only designs its cars but also produces its own batteries (a relationship-specific asset), develops its own software, and sells its cars through its own showrooms (forward integration). This gives Tesla unparalleled control over its technology, quality, and customer experience, which it views as a key competitive advantage.

Examples of the "Buy" Decision (Outsourcing):

  • Apple and Foxconn: This is the classic modern example. Apple's core competencies are design, software development, and marketing. It "buys" manufacturing services from Foxconn, which has enormous economies of scale in electronics assembly. This allows Apple to remain lean and focus on what it does best.
  • J.C. Penney and TAL Apparel: The article "Made to Measure: Invisible Supplier Has Penney's Shirts All Buttoned Up" shows a sophisticated "buy" relationship. Penney doesn't just buy shirts from TAL; it outsources its entire inventory management for dress shirts. TAL uses point-of-sale data from Penney's stores to decide how many shirts to make and ships them directly to each store. Penney benefits from TAL's specialized expertise and efficiency, reducing its own inventory costs to zero.
  • Example of the Hold-Up Problem: General Motors and Fisher Body (1920s): This is a historical textbook case. Fisher Body was the sole supplier of car bodies to GM. As demand for cars boomed, GM worried that Fisher Body would use its monopoly position to "hold them up" by raising prices. To eliminate this risk, GM acquired Fisher Body in 1926, vertically integrating its supply chain.

3. Important Words and Their Meanings

  • Vertical Integration: The combination in one company of two or more stages of production normally operated by separate companies.
  • Make-or-Buy Decision: A firm's strategic choice between producing an item in-house or buying it from an outside supplier.
  • Vertical Chain: The sequence of stages in the production process, from the initial sourcing of raw materials to the final sale to the consumer.
  • Upstream / Downstream: Terms describing the position in the vertical chain. Upstream activities happen earlier in the process (e.g., raw materials); downstream activities happen later (e.g., retail).
  • Transaction Costs: The costs associated with using the market, including search, negotiation, contracting, monitoring, and enforcement costs.
  • Relationship-Specific Asset: An investment that is significantly more valuable in a specific relationship than it would be in any alternative use.
  • Hold-Up Problem: A situation where one party to a contract exploits the other party's relationship-specific investment to renegotiate the terms in their favor.
  • Quasi-Rent: The extra profit a firm gets from using an asset in a specific relationship compared to the profit it would get from its next-best alternative use. It represents what the firm stands to lose if the relationship ends, making it vulnerable to a hold-up.

4. Equations and How to Calculate

Conceptual Framework: Net Economic Value (NEV)

The decision can be framed as a cost-benefit analysis. A firm should choose the option with the higher net value.

Formula: NEV(Make vs. Buy) = (Value from Buying - Costs of Buying) - (Value from Making - Costs of Making)

Costs of Buying (Market Costs): Price Paid to Supplier + Transaction Costs

Costs of Making (Internal Costs): Internal Production Cost + Bureaucratic/Agency Costs

The decision becomes complex because Transaction Costs and Bureaucratic Costs are hard to quantify. The Hold-Up Problem can be thought of as a major potential Transaction Cost.

Calculating Quasi-Rent (The Stake in a Hold-Up)

This formula helps quantify a firm's vulnerability to a hold-up.

Formula: Quasi-Rent = Profit from the best-case use (the specific relationship) - Profit from the next-best alternative use

How to Use It: A large quasi-rent indicates a high degree of asset specificity and a high risk of a hold-up.

Calculation Example:

A supplier invests in equipment to make custom parts for Ford. If the Ford contract proceeds as planned, the supplier will earn a profit of $5 million.
If Ford backs out of the deal at the last minute, the supplier can re-tool its equipment and sell generic parts on the open market, but it will only earn a profit of $500,000.
The supplier's quasi-rent is:
Quasi-Rent = $5,000,000 - $500,000 = $4,500,000
Interpretation: The supplier has $4.5 million at stake in this specific relationship. This is the maximum amount it stands to lose if it gets "held up" by Ford trying to renegotiate the price. Because this amount is so large, the supplier faces significant risk, and vertical integration (Ford making the parts itself) might be a more efficient solution for the industry.

Practical Factors Influencing Vertical Boundaries

The decision to make (integrate) or buy (use the market) is also driven by practical considerations:

Reasons to "Buy" (Market Usage):

Firms may choose to buy inputs to exploit scale or learning economies achieved by independent suppliers. For instance, if an input is subject to significant economies of scale, a dedicated supplier like Goodyear (making 120 million tires) is better positioned to capture those efficiencies than a firm like Toyota (needing 40 million) making them only for internal use. Using the market also imposes discipline; competition forces suppliers to remain efficient.

Reasons to "Make" (Integration):

  • Reliability of Supply: If external supply is unreliable (e.g., a specific hop type versus generic corn), integration may be preferred.
  • Quality Control: If quality is unreliable or difficult to measure (e.g., an accounting audit versus a visible piece of furniture), integration might be a solution. Companies like Matsushita integrated supervision by paying for a dedicated furnace to ensure quality of steel blocks for their compressors.
  • Physical Production Efficiencies: Some modern processes benefit from "cheek-to-jowl" production and joint ownership, such as combining molten aluminum processing with downstream fabrication to avoid heating and cooling materials multiple times.
  • Capturing Value/Profit: Corporations sometimes integrate upstream to insulate themselves from commodity price fluctuations (ArcelorMittal buying iron-ore mines) or downstream to capture higher profit margins associated with specialized parts and lucrative service revenue (Boeing and Airbus making more parts in-house due to supplier consolidation).

Analogy:

The Vertical Boundaries decision is like deciding whether to make a specialized tool in your garage versus buying one from a professional hardware store. If the tool is generic (low specificity, high scale economies in production, like a simple screwdriver), you buy it because the market is efficient and cheap (low transaction costs, high scale benefits). If the tool is highly specialized (high asset specificity, critical to your task, like a unique stamping die only useful for your product), you might make it yourself. This is because if you relied on a supplier, they would incur high risk (if the relationship fails) or they might "hold up" your project once you've committed to their specialized output, making the tool-making transaction costs too high.

Lesson 2c: Problem Questions and Solutions

5) Explain why most car companies produce their own engines but buy mirrors from independent suppliers. (16 points)

Question: Explain why most car companies produce their own engines but buy mirrors from independent suppliers. (16 points)

Solution:

This decision is based on the concepts of core competency, asset specificity, and transaction costs. Car companies make engines because they are a core, highly specific component, while they buy mirrors because they are a standardized, non-core component where specialized suppliers have a cost advantage.

Why They MAKE Engines:
  • Core Competency & Brand Identity: The engine (or powertrain) is fundamental to a car's performance, feel, and brand image. It is a key differentiator in the market. Outsourcing the "heart" of the car would mean giving up a critical source of competitive advantage.
  • High Asset Specificity: Engines are designed specifically for a particular car model. The tools, machinery, and employee skills required to produce them are highly specialized and not easily repurposed. This high specificity makes outsourcing risky, as the car company could become dependent on a single supplier (the "hold-up problem").
  • Complex Coordination: The engine's design is deeply integrated with the car's chassis, transmission, and software. Managing this level of design and engineering coordination with an outside firm would be extremely complex and costly.
Why They BUY Mirrors:
  • Low Asset Specificity: While mirrors must fit the car's design, they are relatively simple, standardized components. Many suppliers can produce them to a given specification.
  • Supplier Economies of Scale: A supplier like Magna or Gentex specializes in making mirrors for dozens of car companies. They produce at a massive scale, allowing them to achieve far lower production costs than a single car company could if it produced mirrors only for its own vehicles.
  • Low Transaction Costs: It is easy to write a contract for mirrors, verify their quality, and switch suppliers if necessary. This makes the transaction simple and low-risk.

6) You are hired as a consultant for a mid-sized producer of outdoor apparel (backpacks, hiking clothing, etc). Over lunch, the CEO explains to you "About three years ago, we just started making our own zippers, buttons, and some of the fabrics we use in our products. So now we keep those profits instead of giving them away to some supplier." Evaluate this argument. (17 points)

Question: You are hired as a consultant for a mid-sized producer of outdoor apparel (backpacks, hiking clothing, etc). Over lunch, the CEO explains to you "About three years ago, we just started making our own zippers, buttons, and some of the fabrics we use in our products. So now we keep those profits instead of giving them away to some supplier." Evaluate this argument. (17 points)

Solution:

The CEO's argument that by making their own components they "keep those profits instead of giving them away to some supplier" is fundamentally flawed. This is a common business mistake known as the "make-or-buy fallacy." My evaluation would be to advise the CEO that this strategy is likely increasing the company's costs, not saving them money.

Here is the evaluation of the argument:

  1. It Ignores Comparative Advantage and Specialization: The supplier who makes zippers and fabrics is a specialist. Through specialization, focused expertise, and economies of scale (producing for many apparel companies), they can almost certainly produce these components at a much lower cost than the apparel company can. The apparel company's core competency is designing and marketing outdoor gear, not manufacturing zippers.
  2. It Confuses the Supplier's Price with the Company's Cost: The CEO is assuming that the supplier's profit margin is a direct saving for his company if they produce the items themselves. However, it is highly probable that the supplier's price (which includes their profit) is still lower than the apparel company's internal cost of production. For example, the company might spend $0.80 to make a zipper that a supplier could sell them for $0.50 (of which $0.10 is the supplier's profit). In this case, the company is not "keeping the profit"; it is losing $0.30 on every zipper it makes.
  3. It Overlooks Opportunity Cost: The capital, labor, and factory space now dedicated to making generic components could have been used to improve the company's core business—designing better backpacks, developing innovative materials, or expanding its marketing efforts. The true cost of making the zippers includes the lost opportunity to invest those resources more profitably elsewhere.

Conclusion: The CEO's decision was likely based on incorrect logic. The company is probably now spending more to create these components inefficiently than it previously spent to buy them from an efficient, specialized supplier. The focus should not be on the supplier's profit but on the total cost. I would advise a "make-or-buy" analysis that compares the full internal cost of production against the market price from suppliers to determine the most cost-effective strategy.

VI Vertical Integration The firm chooses to "make" the input itself, performing activities within the firm that are upstream or downstream along the vertical chain. Examples of firms recently moving toward VI include Oracle, GM, and Boeing.
Make-or-Buy Make-or-Buy Decision The central question of vertical boundaries: deciding whether to perform an activity within the firm or purchase it from an independent firm in the market. This decision exists on a continuum ranging from arm's-length market transactions (less integrated) to performing activity internally (more integrated).
P * Expected Price (or price per unit) The price expected from the buyer (e.g., Ford) when calculating Rent and Quasi-rent in the context of relationship-specific investment examples.
C Cost (specifically variable cost per unit) The average variable cost incurred by the supplier (e.g., cup holder factory). Used in the formula for Rent.
I Initial Fixed Investment (sunk cost) The irreversible upfront capital expenditure required for production (e.g., building a specialized factory). Once undertaken, this investment is a sunk cost.
P ' Second-Best Price (or price in alternative use) The expected unit price the supplier can obtain from its next-best alternative use if the primary deal fails (e.g., selling cups to jobbers instead of Ford).
Rent Rent (Economic Profit) The profit expected from the transaction before the investment is sunk. Calculated as the total revenue minus total cost, including the cost of investment (I). Formula used in context: 1,000,000(P * –C)–I.
Quasi−rent Quasi-rent The extra profit a firm earns above the profit it could get in its next-best alternative use. Formula used in context: 1,000,000(P * –P '). Quasi-rents represent the amount subject to opportunistic behavior once an asset is specialized.
RSI Relative Specific Investment The proportion of the investment (I) that cannot be recovered if the primary relationship is terminated. Formula used in context: RSI=[I−1,000,000(P ' −C)]/I.
FASB Financial Accounting Standards Board The organization that, as of 2016, was grappling with updating rules for recognizing the growing importance of intangible assets (like brands, data, and algorithms). The valuation of intangibles affects the full economic cost/benefit calculation of integration.
