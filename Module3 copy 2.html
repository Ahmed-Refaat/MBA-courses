Lesson 3a: Market Structure and Perfect Competition

Lesson 3a covers Competitors and Competition, beginning with methods for identifying competitors and defining the relevant market, and concluding with an analysis of the Perfect Competition market structure model.

Competitor Identification and Market Definition

Managers must carefully identify their competitors and the features that define their markets. A firm's competition can vary significantly across different input and output markets, and geographically.

The Basics of Competitor Identification

Antitrust agencies, such as the U.S. Department of Justice (DOJ), evaluate whether potential mergers would create enough market power to allow the merged firm to raise prices and earn excessive profits. The DOJ uses a simple conceptual guideline to identify competitors: a competitor is a firm whose product significantly constrains a given firm's ability to increase price.

Putting Competitor Identification into Practice

The SSNIP criterion (Small but Significant Non-transitory Increase in Price) is a common method used to identify competitors, particularly concerning substitutes.

  • SSNIP Criterion Details: The criterion suggests that two firms (X and Y) are direct competitors if a price increase by one causes enough customers to buy the product of the other to make the price increase unprofitable.
  • Substitutes: If products X and Y are substitutes, raising the price of X will increase the quantity demanded of Y. This relationship can be quantified using cross-price elasticity of demand.

SSNIP Criterion

Small but Significant Non-transitory Increase in Price - A method used to identify competitors. If a price increase by one firm causes enough customers to switch to another firm to make the price increase unprofitable, then the two firms are direct competitors.

Cross-Price Elasticity of Demand

ηQX,PY = % Change in Quantity of X / % Change in Price of Y

Positive cross-price elasticity indicates that two goods are substitutes.

Luxury Cars Example

When Mercedes began reducing its prices in the early 1990s, the resulting market share loss for Lexus and Acura led Mercedes to view non-German luxury car brands (Lexus, Cadillac, Volvo) as competitors.

Postal Services Example

The U.S. Postal Service (USPS) faces competition not just from private delivery services (like FedEx), but also from electronic alternatives (email, internet) for mail delivery, demonstrating how substitutes can span different industries.

Geographic Competitor Identification

Identifying competitors often requires defining the geographic scope of competition.

Sporting Goods Example

To identify competitors for Bay City Sports, a sporting goods store in San Francisco, an analysis of where customers live and shop is necessary to define the relevant trade area, distinguishing between direct and indirect competitors.

Perfect Competition

Perfect competition is characterized by features that lead to intense price pressure, driving prices down toward marginal costs.

Characteristics of Perfect Competition

  • Many sellers: There are many sellers.
  • Homogeneous product: The product is perceived to be homogeneous.
  • Consumers are price perceptive: Consumers are aware of prices and lack excess capacity.
  • Easy entry and exit: There are few barriers to entering or leaving the market.

Pricing and Profit Outcomes

  • Price Taker: A firm in perfect competition is a price taker; if it sets a price above the market price, it sells nothing, and if it sets a price below the market price, it needlessly sacrifices revenue.
  • Profit Maximization: The firm's optimal condition for profit maximization is when the percentage contribution margin (PCM) is zero. The optimal short-term price is the marginal cost (P=MC).
  • Long-Run Equilibrium: In the long run, firms earn zero economic profits, with the price equaling average cost (P=AC).

Profit Maximization in Perfect Competition

P = MC (Price equals Marginal Cost)

In the long run: P = AC (Price equals Average Cost)

Percentage Contribution Margin

PCM = (P − MC) / P

In perfect competition, PCM = 0 for profit maximization.

Market Dynamics Examples

These examples illustrate how perfect competition drives prices toward marginal costs and leads to zero economic profits in the long run.

U.S. Domestic Airline Industry (1990s)

The industry experienced ups and downs, declining in the 1990s but recovering in the mid-1990s. When demand for air travel declined and many carriers had empty seats, the marginal cost of flying an empty seat was negligible. Carriers slashed prices, resulting in an industry loss exceeding $4 billion in 1992.

Cobalt and Lithium Markets

These metals, essential for electric-vehicle batteries, are treated as commodities. Investors anticipated supply shortages, driving prices up in 2017. However, the rush by miner companies to capitalize on excitement led to steady production and a tumble in prices starting in 2018, illustrating that robust supply can quickly undermine price surges in commodity markets. For example, Cobalt prices fell over 30% in 2019, despite rising global electric vehicle sales.

Pecan Business (Commodity Market)

The U.S. pecan industry faces market fragmentation (pecans grow across several states and 20-30% are from wild groves) and lacks the grower cartels that coordinate supply and pricing for walnuts and almonds. Strong Chinese demand (starting around 2007/2009) combined with finite supply pushed prices up dramatically (e.g., price of a pound in the shell nearly doubled in three years). Shellers (middlemen) struggled because they faced volatile prices and difficulty securing nuts before Chinese buyers. This market demonstrates price-taking behavior among growers who sell to the highest bidder.

Greek Olive Oil Industry

Greece is the world's third-largest producer, but only 4% of branded olive oil sold worldwide is Greek. Most Greek producers focus on selling bulk oil, which yields quick cash but low margins (as little as 10 to 20 euro cents a liter for a middleman). This suggests they are operating in a highly competitive, commodity-like bulk market where lack of investment, bureaucracy, and scarce bank lending prevent them from transitioning to the higher-margin branded market.

Restaurant Industry (Oversupply)

The U.S. restaurant industry experienced a significant shakeout due to oversupply; the number of restaurants grew faster than the population between 2006 and 2014. Increased competition from rival sources of prepared meals (like meal-kit delivery) exacerbated the problem, leading to bankruptcies and restaurant closures. This environment indicates intense competition, typical of market structures where entry is easy and returns are strained. In response, many chains slashed prices to fill seats, but this only accelerated the cycle of low margins and exits, reinforcing the price-taking nature of competitive markets.

Electric Vehicle Market Disappointment

The electric vehicle (EV) market was touted as a revolutionary "gold rush" in the late 2010s, with soaring demand for battery materials like lithium and cobalt driving prices to record highs. Investors and miners rushed in, anticipating sustained shortages. However, by 2018-2019, slower-than-expected EV adoption and increased supply led to sharp price declines (e.g., cobalt prices fell over 30% in 2019 despite rising global EV sales). This boom-bust cycle exemplifies how commodity markets operate under perfect competition: easy entry attracts overinvestment, leading to oversupply, price collapses, and zero long-run profits for producers who remain price takers.

Greek Olive Oil Industry

Greece, the world's third-largest olive oil producer, struggles to capture value in a highly competitive global market. Despite producing high-quality oil, only 4% of worldwide branded olive oil sales are Greek. Most production is sold in bulk at low margins (as little as 10-20 euro cents per liter), due to industry fragmentation (pecans grow across states with no coordination), bureaucracy, and limited access to branding and distribution channels. This illustrates perfect competition where producers are price takers, unable to earn economic profits without differentiation or market power, and highlights the challenges of transitioning from commodity bulk markets to premium branded segments.

Lesson 3b: Pricing with Market Power

Lesson 3b explores pricing strategies employed by firms that possess market power—the ability to raise price without losing all customers to competition. This lesson details profit maximization principles, the benchmark case of single price per unit, and methods of price discrimination used to capture potential consumer surplus.

Market Structures with Market Power

  • Monopoly: A market structure where a firm is the sole seller, and effective barriers prevent entry. The demand curve faced by the firm is the industry demand curve.
  • Monopolistic Competition: A hybrid structure with many firms, selling differentiated products, and having free entry. Although firms have market power (due to product differentiation), competition prevents them from earning economic profits in the long run.

Benchmark Case – Single Price per Unit

Firms with market power face a downward-sloping demand curve, meaning they must lower the price to sell more units.

Profit-maximising rule

MR = MC

A firm maximizes profit by setting the quantity where marginal revenue equals marginal cost, then read the price off the demand curve at that quantity.

Pricing Rule (Monopoly)

For a single price per unit, the optimal price (P*) is determined by the demand curve at the profit-maximizing quantity.

Inverse-elasticity pricing rule

P* = MC* ÷ (1 – 1/η*)

The optimal price markup above marginal cost depends on the elasticity of demand (η). The optimal monopolistic price can be expressed using the inverse elasticity pricing rule. The more elastic demand, the smaller the markup.

Price Discrimination

Price discrimination occurs when a firm charges different prices to customers not related to differences in production or distribution costs. This requires heterogeneous consumer demands (different price elasticities).

1st-degree (Personalised)

Charging each customer the maximum amount they are willing to pay (their reservation price). This extracts the maximum consumer surplus and is theoretically the most efficient outcome.

3rd-degree (Group)

Segmenting the market into groups and setting a different price for each group. Examples include different prices charged to students versus non-students for college tuition, or varying prices based on age, income, or time of purchase.

Two-part tariff

Pricing that requires customers to pay an upfront fee (capital charge) for the right to buy the product, and then a price per unit (usage charge), often set at or near marginal cost. This strategy can capture surplus from customers with high demands.

Gillette razor & blades

Gillette sells razors (the capital good) and replacement blades (the consumables). This is a classic two-part tariff strategy.

Real-World Pricing Stories

Parker Hannifin – 800 000 SKUs

Abandoned cost-plus (cost + 35 %) for “strategic pricing”: classified parts A→Special; raised niche items 3 %-60 %; added ≈ $200 m operating income.

Copy

Pfizer Ibrance – $9 850 / month

Price just below $10 k threshold that triggers insurer red-tape; balances revenue vs. access; competitor Afinitor later raised price but Pfizer held line to maintain payer goodwill.

Gillette vs. Dollar Shave Club

Start-ups eroded Gillette’s >70 % share to 54 % (2010-16); Gillette cut list prices up to 20 % to defend volume—showing even strong brands can lose pricing power. In response, Gillette introduced subscription services and bundled offerings to retain customers, demonstrating how firms with market power must adapt pricing strategies to counter disruptive low-cost entrants.

Anatomy of Drug Pricing

Pharmaceutical companies often charge high prices for new drugs, justified by R&D costs (averaging $2.6 billion per drug) and patent protections that grant temporary monopoly power. However, critics argue this leads to excessive profits, with drugs like Sovaldi priced at $84,000 for a 12-week course. This pricing reflects market power from patents, but also sparks debates on access and affordability, highlighting the trade-offs in pricing with market power.

Seeking Perfect Prices: Dynamic Pricing Strategies

Firms use advanced analytics to implement dynamic pricing, adjusting prices based on demand, time, and customer segments. Airlines and hotels employ yield management to maximize revenue, charging higher prices during peak times. E-commerce platforms like Amazon use algorithms for real-time price adjustments. This approach captures more consumer surplus and optimizes profits, but requires careful consideration of customer perceptions and regulatory constraints.

Lesson 3c: Economics of Strategy – Game Theory

How oligopolists make decisions when payoffs depend on rivals’ actions, and why individually rational choices can yield jointly poor outcomes.

Oligopoly & Strategic Interaction

Oligopoly defined

Few large firms, high barriers, mutual inter-dependence; best analysed with simultaneous-move or sequential games.

Key concepts

  • Strategy profile: set of choices by all players.
  • Dominant strategy: best for a player no matter what rivals do.
  • Nash equilibrium: no player can gain by unilaterally deviating.
  • Prisoners’ / cartel dilemma: equilibrium payoffs < cooperative payoffs.

Game-Theory Toolkit

Normal-form example (Boeing vs. Airbus)

Boeing High Boeing Low
Airbus High 750, 750 0, 1 000
Airbus Low 1 000, 0 500, 500

Low-price is a dominant strategy → both price low (500, 500) even though (750, 750) is jointly better.

Copy

Cartel instability

Each member’s temptation to cheat:

πcheat > πcooperate ⇒ cartel collapses without enforcement.

Explains OPEC squabbles, lysine, vitamins, etc.

Strategic Mistakes & Tacit Games

Airbus A380 – mis-read market trend

Bet on hub congestion & 4-engine jumbos; ignored twin-engine efficiency (B-787, A-350). Program cancelled after 251 deliveries; break-even ~420.

Copy

Cruise lines – capacity commitment trap

Ships ordered years ahead; demand shocks → 2-for-1 deals, 70 % discounts to fill berths.

Frontier vs. United at Denver hub

Frontier flew thin schedules, off-bank times, medium fares—high enough to avoid United’s capacity wrath, low enough to skim price-sensitive travellers. Classic "puppy-dog" strategy: look small & non-threatening. This illustrates how entrants can use strategic positioning to avoid retaliation in oligopolistic markets.

Boeing vs. Airbus Confrontation

The duopoly in large commercial aircraft has led to intense strategic interactions, including pricing wars and subsidies. Airbus's entry challenged Boeing's dominance, leading to retaliatory pricing and government interventions. This rivalry exemplifies how oligopolists engage in game-theoretic strategies, where actions like capacity expansions or price cuts are designed to deter entry or gain market share, often resulting in suboptimal outcomes for both firms.

Cruise Industry Overcapacity

Cruise lines invested heavily in mega-ships, anticipating demand growth, but faced overcapacity when demand slowed. Lines like Carnival and Royal Caribbean slashed prices with 2-for-1 deals and deep discounts to fill berths, leading to price wars and reduced profitability. This demonstrates the prisoner's dilemma in oligopolies, where individual capacity decisions lead to collective harm, exacerbated by long lead times in ship construction.

Upstarts' Entry Tactics in Oligopolies

New entrants in concentrated markets often use asymmetric strategies to challenge incumbents. For instance, low-cost carriers like Southwest disrupted legacy airlines by targeting underserved routes and offering no-frills service. In tech, companies like Netflix entered the entertainment oligopoly by focusing on streaming, avoiding direct competition with cable giants initially. These tactics highlight how game theory informs entry strategies, emphasizing speed, niche targeting, and signaling to deter aggressive responses from established players.

Keywords & Glossary

Key Terms

Market Structure

The characteristics of a market defined by number of buyers/sellers, product differentiation, and entry conditions.

Perfect Competition

Market structure with many sellers, homogeneous products, and easy entry/exit, resulting in firms being price takers.

Market Power

The ability of a firm to raise its price without losing all its customers.

Monopoly

Market structure where a single firm supplies the entire market; effective barriers to entry exist.

Monopolistic Competition

Hybrid market structure with many firms, differentiated products, and free entry.

Oligopoly

Market structure dominated by a few large firms; actions of one firm heavily impact others.

Game Theory

General analysis of strategic interaction, used to predict outcomes when decisions depend on rivals' actions.

Nash Equilibrium

An outcome where no firm has incentive to unilaterally change its action, given the action of the rival.

Dominant Strategy

A strategy that is optimal for a firm regardless of what its rival does.

SSNIP Criterion

Small but Significant Non-transitory Increase in Price; used in competitor identification and market definition.

Price Discrimination

Charging different prices to different customers for reasons unrelated to cost differences.

Cartel

Formal agreement among firms to cooperate in setting price and output levels.
