Lesson 2b: Horizontal Boundaries (Economies of Scale and Scope)

This section focuses on the advantages and disadvantages of operating at different scales (how much output is produced) and scopes (how many different products are produced), derived primarily from BDSS Ch 2.

1. Detailed Explanation

The Horizontal Boundaries of a firm define how much of the market it serves and what range of products and services it offers. The key drivers of these boundaries are economies of scale and economies of scope.

Economies of Scale ("Bigger is Better in Volume")

This is the core concept that long-run average cost per unit of production decreases as the total quantity of output increases. A firm that can achieve economies of scale has a powerful competitive advantage over smaller rivals. There are several reasons why this occurs:

Spreading of Fixed Costs & Indivisibilities

This is the most common source. Many inputs are indivisible, meaning they cannot be scaled down below a minimum size, creating a fixed cost. A large production volume spreads this fixed cost over more units, lowering the average fixed cost per unit.

Business Context: A $50 million factory, a $300 million R&D project for a new drug, or a $5 million national advertising campaign all represent large, indivisible fixed costs. The firm that can sell the most units will have the lowest per-unit cost allocated to these essential investments.

Increased Productivity of Variable Inputs (Specialization)

Larger firms can support a greater division of labor. Workers can specialize in specific tasks and become highly proficient, leading to increased speed and quality. This is the principle behind the assembly line.

Physical Properties of Production (The Cube-Square Rule)

This engineering principle states that as the volume of a container (like a pipe or a tank) increases, its surface area increases by a smaller proportion. Since production capacity is often related to volume and cost is often related to surface area (materials needed), this creates a natural economy of scale.

Business Context: Doubling the diameter of an oil pipeline more than doubles its carrying capacity but does not double the material cost to build it, thus lowering the transportation cost per barrel.

Diseconomies of Scale ("Too Big to Succeed")

Eventually, average costs may begin to rise as a firm gets too large. This happens due to:

  • Bureaucracy & Coordination Costs: Larger organizations become more complex, slowing down communication and decision-making.
  • Agency Problems: It becomes harder for managers to monitor employee effort, leading to potential slack.
  • Spreading Specialized Resources Too Thin: A star CEO, a brilliant engineering team, or a celebrity chef cannot be in two places at once. As the firm expands, these key resources get diluted.

Economies of Scope ("Better Together in Variety")

This refers to the cost savings a firm achieves by producing a variety of different products using the same resources. It's cheaper to produce two or more different products together in one firm than it is to produce them in separate firms.

Business Context: Economies of scope are the economic driver behind diversification and brand extensions. They occur when a company can leverage a core competence, a shared input, or a strong brand name across multiple product lines. A company might use shared machinery, a common distribution network, or a single marketing campaign for multiple products.

2. Examples

Examples of Economies of Scale:

  • Automotive Manufacturing (Toyota, Ford): The massive fixed cost of a car manufacturing plant (robotics, assembly lines) is spread over millions of vehicles produced, dramatically lowering the cost per car.
  • Software (Microsoft): The cost to develop Microsoft Windows is billions of dollars (a fixed cost). However, the marginal cost to produce and sell one more digital license is nearly zero. This creates immense economies of scale.
  • Logistics (Amazon, Walmart): These companies invest billions in massive, automated fulfillment centers and sophisticated logistics networks. This high fixed cost allows them to achieve a lower cost per package shipped than any smaller competitor could. The article "Amazon Robots Get Ready for Christmas" highlights this, showing how the fixed investment in Kiva robots increases efficiency and lowers the variable cost of labor per item picked.

Examples of Economies of Scope:

  • Consumer Goods (Procter & Gamble): P&G leverages its world-class R&D, brand management expertise, and distribution relationships with retailers across a wide array of products, from Tide laundry detergent to Crest toothpaste.
  • Honda: Honda uses its core competence in engine manufacturing to produce cars, motorcycles, lawnmowers, and generators, all sharing technology and brand reputation.
  • Chicken and Alligator Farming: The article "New Role for the Gator: Chicken Farmer's Friend" provides a unique example. Dead chickens, which are a waste-disposal cost for a chicken farmer, become a free food input for raising alligators. By combining these two seemingly unrelated businesses, the firm creates an economy of scope.

3. Important Words and Their Meanings

  • Horizontal Boundaries: The scope of a firm's activities, covering the variety of products it produces and the volume of its output.
  • Economies of Scale: The reduction in long-run average cost per unit that arises from an increase in the volume of production.
  • Diseconomies of Scale: The increase in long-run average cost per unit that can occur when a firm becomes too large and inefficient.
  • Minimum Efficient Scale (MES): The lowest level of output at which a firm can minimize its long-run average costs. Reaching MES is often critical for survival in competitive industries.
  • Economies of Scope: The cost savings that result when a firm produces a variety of products instead of specializing in just one.
  • Indivisibility: An input's inability to be scaled down below a certain minimum size, leading to a fixed cost.
  • Average Cost (AC): The total cost of production divided by the total number of units produced (TC/Q).
  • Cube-Square Rule: A physical principle stating that as an object's volume increases by a certain proportion, its surface area increases by a smaller proportion.

4. Equations and How to Calculate

Calculating Average Cost to Identify Economies of Scale:

The primary formula is for Average Cost (AC).

Formula: AC = Total Cost (TC) / Quantity (Q)

How to Use It: You have economies of scale if, as you increase your production quantity (Q), your average cost per unit (AC) goes down.

Calculation Example:

A company produces 10,000 units at a Total Cost of $500,000.
AC₁ = $500,000 / 10,000 units = $50 per unit
It then increases production to 50,000 units, and its Total Cost rises to $2,000,000.
AC₂ = $2,000,000 / 50,000 units = $40 per unit
Since the Average Cost has fallen from $50 to $40 per unit, the firm is experiencing economies of scale over this range of output.

The Formal Condition for Economies of Scope:

This is more of a theoretical test than a simple calculation, but it defines the concept mathematically.

Formula: A firm exhibits economies of scope if:
TC(Qx, Qy) < TC(Qx, 0) + TC(0, Qy)

Explanation of Terms:

  • TC(Qx, Qy): The total cost for a single firm to produce quantity Qx of good X and quantity Qy of good Y together.
  • TC(Qx, 0): The total cost if a specialized firm produces only quantity Qx of good X.
  • TC(0, Qy): The total cost if a different specialized firm produces only quantity Qy of good Y.

How to Interpret It: The formula states that economies of scope exist if the cost of producing two goods jointly is less than the sum of the costs of producing them separately in two different firms.

Calculation Example (from the textbook):

Cost to produce 100 million message notes alone: TC(100m, 0) = $55 million
Cost to produce 600 million rolls of tape alone: TC(0, 600m) = $220 million
Total cost if produced separately = $55m + 220m = $275 million
Cost to produce both together in one firm: TC(100m, 600m) = $245 million
Since $245 million < $275 million, this production process exhibits strong economies of scope.

Main Stream

Indivisibilities and Spreading Fixed Costs

Large lumpy assets—think Boeing 787 simulators or semiconductor fabs—come in one size only; you cannot buy "half a clean-room". Because these fixed costs are indivisible, the average cost curve plunges as output climbs: the same $100 million wafer plant baked into 1 million chips adds $100 per unit, but spread over 10 million wafers it drops to $10. Indivisibilities therefore push firms toward giant scale, explaining why global chip-makers cluster in a handful of mega-fabs rather than thousands of boutique foundries.

Inventories (Lower Stock-outs)

Scale grants statistical economies of inventory: a national e-commerce giant holding safety stock in one regional "mega-hub" faces less absolute variability than ten local warehouses. With higher turnover and lower stock-out risk, the firm slashes emergency freight, lost sales and customer-service costs—another subtle but powerful source of declining average cost as volume rises.

Average Cost (AC) Declines as Output (Q) Increases

The signature shape of modern manufacturing: AC slides downward because fixed costs are diluted, variable inputs are used more efficiently, and diseconomies have not yet kicked in. This downward-sloping region is the economist's playground for pricing strategy: firms can under-cut rivals, gain share, and still raise margin simply by climbing the volume curve.

Cube-Square Rule (Physical Properties)

Mother Nature loves scale: double the linear dimensions of a spherical LNG tank and volume rises eight-fold while surface area only four-folds. Because heat loss, steel cost and welding labour grow with surface area, energy per unit cargo plummets—a physics-based EOS that rewards oil majors for building Q-Max ships the size of small towns.

Specialization and Increased Productivity

Adam Smith's pin factory on steroids: at 50,000 units per day Tesla dedicates entire robots to one bolt, slashing cycle time and rework. Greater throughput justifies narrower tasks, which lifts learning speed and reduces error rates—a self-reinforcing loop where scale begets speed, speed begets lower unit cost, and lower cost begets still more scale.

Learning by Doing (Learning Curve)

Every doubling of cumulative output can shave 15–25 % off unit cost in semiconductor fabs—not because the plant gets bigger, but because engineers tweak recipes, reduce defect rates and speed yield ramps. The learning curve is path-dependent: first movers lock in a cost edge that late entrants must buy their way down, creating persistent scale-based moats.

Purchasing (Quantity Discounts)

Volume concentrates bargaining power: when Walmart orders one billion toothpaste tubes, suppliers voluntarily slide down their own cost curves and pass part of the rebate to the retailer. These back-of-the-invoice discounts are pure margin for the buyer, turning procurement into a profit centre and flattening rivals who lack the clout to command the same terms.

Advertising / Marketing (Cost per Potential Customer Falls)

A $5 million Super-Bowl spot reaching 100 million viewers costs 5 ¢ per viewer—cheaper than printing and mailing a coupon. Scale transforms fixed marketing outlays into tiny per-unit levies, letting Coca-Cola outspend craft cola while spending less per can sold—a media economies-of-scale that reinforces brand dominance.

Optimal Plant Size (Minimum LRAC)

Engineers design single-train ammonia plants at 1,500 tonnes/day because thermal efficiency peaks at that pressure-vessel diameter; anything smaller wastes heat, anything larger stresses metallurgy. This technological sweet spot becomes the lowest point on the long-run average cost curve—the optimal plant size every entrant must replicate to survive price wars.

Minimum Efficient Scale (MES)

The smallest output at which LRAC bottoms out—for passenger jets roughly 400 aircraft/year (Airbus A320 family). Produce below MES and unit cost skyrockets; produce at or above MES and you match the cost leader. MES sets the admission ticket for an industry.

MES Relative to Market Size Determines Market Structure

If global demand is only 500 units and MES = 400, the planet cannot profitably host more than one firm—a natural monopoly (wide-body jets). If MES = 1 % of market, dozens of boutiques can coexist (craft beer). Thus MES/market ratio is the silent architect of concentration ratios.

Cargo Ships (Use Mega-Ships for Lower Cost per Container)

Maersk's Triple-E class carries 18,000 TEU at 13 % less fuel per box than 13,000 TEU vessels—cube-square rule at sea. Port fees, crew wages and bunker fuel are spread over more containers, pushing average cost per TEU below any rival route and locking in hub-and-spoke geography.

↔️ Horizontal Boundaries of the Firm (Lesson 2b)

How much output (not how many products) the firm chooses to produce before diseconomies outweigh economies. Horizontal expansion stops where LRAC starts to rise—the right-hand boundary of the cost U-shape.

AC Rises as Q Increases (Diseconomies of Scale)

Beyond optimal scale, bureaucracy thickens, communication distorts, and motivation dilutes—each extra unit costs more. This upward-sloping region is the warning flag that size has become a liability, not an asset.

Incentive and Bureaucracy Effects

Layer upon layer of middle management slows decision cycles; performance metrics become noisy, so bonus schemes weaken. Free-rider problems emerge as individual contribution is harder to observe, shrinking marginal productivity and lifting unit cost.

Spreading Specialized Resources Too Thin

A world-class battery chemist who guided 5 projects can barely supervise 50; error rates climb and innovation stalls. Key talent becomes the bottleneck, forcing duplication of high-skill heads—diseconomy via dilution.

Labor Costs (Higher Wages in Larger Firms)

Gigantic firms often unionize or pay efficiency wages to retain 10,000+ workers, pushing wage premium over small rivals. Higher average wage per hour feeds directly into higher average cost per unit once scale exceeds the sweet spot.

Economies of Scope (EOSp)

Producing two products in one plant is cheaper than producing each alone because shared inputs, joint branding and common distribution spread fixed costs wider. EOSp bends the cost curve downward even after EOS is exhausted, giving conglomerates a second wind.

Dairy / Alligator Farming Example (Waste Recycling)

A dairy plant churns milk, cheese and ice cream using the same cold-chain, lab-testing and logistics—one R&D team tweaks fat content for three SKUs. Likewise, alligator farms sell meat, hides and tourist shows, recycling animal waste into pet-food pellets—scope, not scale, shrinks per-product cost.

Lesson 2b: Problem Questions and Solutions

3) Hypermarkets and Economies of Scale/Diseconomies of Scale

Question: In recent decades, many countries have seen the rise of "hypermarkets", enormous retail stores that sell groceries, household goods, hardware, and other products under one roof. What are some possible economies of scale these stores might experience? Are there any possibilities for diseconomies of scale? Explain. (17 points)

Solution:

Hypermarkets can experience significant economies of scale, which means their long-run average costs per unit decrease as their size and output increase. Some possibilities include:

Economies of Scale:
  • Purchasing Power: By buying products in enormous volumes, hypermarkets can negotiate substantial discounts from suppliers, lowering their cost of goods sold.
  • Marketing Economies: A single advertising campaign (e.g., a national TV ad or weekly flyer) can cover a massive sales volume, making the marketing cost per item sold extremely low.
  • Logistical and Distribution Efficiencies: Large chains can invest in highly efficient, centralized warehousing and transportation networks, reducing the cost of stocking and distributing each product.
  • Managerial Specialization: Hypermarkets can afford to hire specialized managers for areas like finance, supply chain management, and marketing, leading to greater operational efficiency.
  • Financial Economies: Large, established corporations can often borrow money at lower interest rates than smaller businesses, reducing their cost of capital for expansion or operations.

Yes, there are also possibilities for diseconomies of scale, where the long-run average costs per unit begin to increase as the firm becomes too large.

Diseconomies of Scale:
  • Coordination and Communication Issues: As the organization grows, it can become bureaucratic and slow. Communication between top management and individual store employees can become ineffective, leading to poor decision-making and a disconnect from customer needs.
  • Worker Alienation: In a massive corporation, employees may feel like a "small cog in a big machine," leading to lower morale, reduced productivity, and poorer customer service.
  • Managerial Inefficiencies: The business may become too large and complex for a single management team to control effectively, leading to waste, duplication of effort, and a lack of clear strategic direction across its many departments and locations.

4) Boeing and Airbus: Labor-to-Capital Ratios

Question: The American company Boeing and the European company Airbus are the two main players in the market for large commercial aircraft production. They produce roughly the same volume of aircraft each year, both use capital and labor to produce their planes, and both have access the similar production technologies. A key difference is that in Europe, labor costs are significantly higher than in the US. Using concepts from this lesson, would you expect these two companies to have the same ratio of labor to capital? Why or why not, and explain. (17 points)

Solution:

No, I would not expect Boeing and Airbus to have the same ratio of labor to capital. I would expect Airbus to have a lower labor-to-capital ratio (i.e., be more capital-intensive) than Boeing.

The core reason is the difference in the relative prices of inputs. The problem states that labor costs are significantly higher in Europe than in the US. In production, firms aim to minimize the cost of producing a certain level of output. When one input (labor) becomes relatively more expensive, a profit-maximizing firm will substitute away from that expensive input and use more of the relatively cheaper input (capital).

Therefore, Airbus, facing high European labor costs, has a strong economic incentive to invest more heavily in automation, robotics, and other capital equipment to reduce its reliance on expensive labor. Boeing, facing relatively cheaper labor in the US, would not have the same level of incentive to make this substitution and would likely use a more labor-intensive production process compared to Airbus. Both companies have access to similar technology, so their different choices in production mix would be driven primarily by the need to minimize costs based on the different input prices they face.
